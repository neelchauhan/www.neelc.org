We've all seen Satya Nadella saying
[AI systems are "overbuilt"](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/microsoft-ceo-says-there-is-an-overbuild-of-ai-systems-dismisses-agi-milestones-as-show-of-progress).
Well, I *worked* at Microsoft and can agree: AI is overhyped.

A lot of AI people are saying GenAI is the next big revolution, the way PCs
were in the 80s, web in the 90s, and mobile in the 2010s. However, there is a
big difference between other technologies and AI.

The dot-com boom was possible on the backs of well-tested technologies, ones
easy to get right. DARPA, Berkeley, AT&amp;T and IEEE perfected the foundations
of the internet. Likewise, carriers and the 3GPP/3GPP2 cemented the cellular
networks while Apple, Samsung and Xiaomi blended consumer electronics with
cellular capabilities.

AI is different: AI gets so many things wrong decades after it was conceived.
The telecom industry is building a road, it's easy to get that right. Apple,
Samsung and Xiaomi building a car, it's easy to get that right also. AI is
building a brain, nobody outside of God (if you believe in one) knows how it
works, and human scientists can only go so far. We don't know how the brain
truly works, so how can we build a computerized brian using Nvidia GPUs and
LLMs? **We can't!**

I am working on a IT business and was suggested to heavily rely on AI,
including to build ads and a FAQ. The AI ads were so terrible it's obviously
AI-generated. But the person working with me was so heavily invested in AI
(they worked in AI before getting fired) they had to drink the AI kool-aid.

That's not to say that AI can't have a future, but it'll not be a revolution
but instead a companion tool for certain tasks. This is similar to how Web3 was
marketed as a "revolution" but instead became an alternative investment class.

Some revolutions have truly taken off: PCs, the internet, and mobile devices.
There are "revolutions" which never took off: VR, the metaverse and Web3 come
to mind. And there are technologies which have some niche like Big Data. For
instance, most companies aren't Big Data companies, but most of Big Tech is.

Case in point: I spent most of my professional life doing Big Data but left
because I felt Big Data is more harmful than good. I'd rather *make less*, in
fact half of what I used to make, than work in surveillance. Sure, there are
some legitimate uses of Big Data but 90% of the use case is surveillance,
including what I worked on.

Big Data has evolved into GenAI, but it's basically sweeping up everyone's
data, ignoring copyright, spending awful lots of money hoping to one day make a
profit. And unlike the web, AI is far from production-ready.

Also Nvidia will probably crash. After all Cisco crashed after the dot-com
bubble and never fully recovered. Maybe Cisco will recover, but years, possibly
decades from now. And I won't be surprised if Nvidia only recovers in the 2050s,
assuming they even survive that long.
